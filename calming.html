Calming Activities

Calming activities can help children manage their emotions, reduce stress, and develop self-regulation skills.




Offering a calming activity in these moments isn’t about stopping behavior — it’s about helping a child’s nervous system settle so they can think, listen, and connect again. Calming tools can also be used proactively, like before bedtime, before school, or after busy outings, to help children reset and feel safe in their bodies.


Breathing techniques help preschoolers calm their bodies when emotions feel big. When children are upset or overstimulated, their breathing often speeds up, which can make it harder to settle. Simple, playful strategies like “smell the flower, blow out the candle,” blowing bubbles, or tracing a slow “breathing mountain” with a finger can help slow the breath and signal safety to the body. With gentle guidance and repetition, these small practices support growing self-regulation skills.

Sensory tools can help children regulate by giving their bodies the input they need to feel organized and safe. When preschoolers are overwhelmed, they often benefit from simple sensory experiences like squeezing playdough, hugging a stuffed animal, wrapping up in a soft blanket, rocking gently, or pushing against a wall. These activities provide calming pressure or movement that helps the nervous system settle. The key is offering tools proactively and modeling how to use them, rather than waiting for big emotions to escalate. Over time, children begin to recognize which sensory supports help them feel calm and in control.



The Science of Calm

When children take slow, deep breaths or move their bodies gently, their nervous system begins to shift from “alert and overwhelmed” to “safe and steady.” Deep breathing sends signals to the brain that it’s okay to relax, slowing the heart rate and helping muscles soften. This is because the body’s calming system — sometimes called the parasympathetic nervous system — turns on when we breathe slowly and steadily. For preschoolers, simple calming activities like blowing bubbles, pretending to smell a flower, or rocking gently can help their bodies regulate before their words can. Over time, these small practices build stronger emotional awareness and self-control.

Calming activities also support emotional regulation by giving children a way to express and process big feelings. When a child is upset, naming their emotions and offering a calming tool can help them feel seen and supported. For example, you might say: “I see you’re feeling really angry right now. Do you want to take some deep breaths with me?”

Calming activities to try Here are some simple, low-prep activities to help your child find calm when they need it most. Remember, the goal isn’t perfection — it’s connection and support. Follow your child’s lead and adjust as needed. Bubble Breaths – Blow bubbles together, encouraging slow, deep breaths to create big, steady bubbles. Flower Smells – Pretend to smell a flower, taking a deep breath in through the nose and blowing out through the mouth like you’re blowing out a candle. Rocking Chair – Sit together in a rocking chair or on a swing and gently rock back and forth to soothe the body. Cozy Corner – Create a small cozy space with pillows and blankets where your child can retreat when they need a break. Body Scan – Guide your child through a simple body scan, asking them to notice how their feet feel, then their legs, tummy, arms, and head — helping them connect with their body sensations.
